# [Original] A Brief History of Human Attention: How We Lost the Ability to Stare at a Tree

> We are the only content consumers on this planet, right?

*Published on 2026-02-25*

> I invite you to notice a fact: that you are focusing on this text, not interrupted by the notification sound of another device in your pocket, not overwhelmed by the to-do list suddenly popping up in your mind. This in itself, in the present moment, is already a small victory.
>
> Countless people are experiencing the same frustration: we seem to be losing the ability for deep reading and long-term focus. But is the problem really that simple? Have we "lost" a certain ability, or have we adapted to a new cognitive environment? If the answer is the former, is this "attention crisis" irreversible? If it is the latter, how should we place our minds in the new wave of technology?

Imagine a scene: in an era before writing was born, a Homo sapiens stands on the African savanna. He is not admiring the scenery, but keeping watch. His breathing and heartbeat are integrated with the surrounding environment. The subtle sound of wind passing through acacia trees, the trajectory of a flock of birds suddenly rising from the distant bushes, the direction of a string of fresh hoof prints under his feet—all of these flood into his senses simultaneously. He doesn't need to deliberately "concentrate" his attention, because alertness itself is an instinct. His attention is like a spread-out net, capturing the pulse of the entire savanna. Aside from keeping watch and hunting, when food has been found, early Homo sapiens had plenty of time to gaze at a tree and observe the texture of its leaves. In that period, attention was a dormant survival tool. Only when a strange noise came from the grass would this tool be instantly activated. The pressure of survival shaped human's extreme sensitivity to dynamic and novel things, while in an absolutely safe environment, the brain defaulted to a low-power idle mode. This aimless blankness happened to provide the necessary space for the evolution of the early mind and the birth of self-consciousness.

Bring your gaze back to this moment. You are sitting in a coffee shop, your phone screen is on, your laptop has hundreds of tabs open, and the terminals inside are running countless Agents. You try to finish reading a public account article: "In the Cyber Age, Teach You Not to Be Anxious". You read the beginning, scroll directly to the bottom, and ask an AI to summarize: "What is this article about?". In the blink of an eye, you exit and subconsciously swipe open TikTok. A fifteen-second short video fails to make you laugh until the last two seconds, and you have already swiped to the next one. You seem to be paying attention to everything at the same time, but nothing stays. Before you swipe through two videos, the notification sound of the Agent task completion pops up. You start to get busy in multiple task interfaces. After issuing a new task, you click on another article... A period of time passes. This feeling is so common that I'm not sure if it can be called a "disease of the times".

But perhaps this is not anyone's fault. Attention has never been a constant function, but a tool that evolves with technology and survival strategies. The distraction we experience today may just be the latest chapter in the long history of human cognitive evolution. In the long years of hunting and gathering, human attention was a "panoramic view". At that time, there were no screens, no books, and not even fixed ridges. Survival required the brain to scan continuously like a radar. A person who focused on tracking a rabbit while ignoring a saber-toothed tiger sneaking in the grass could not pass on his genes. That kind of attention was fluid and open; it was immersed in change rather than stillness, focusing on relationships rather than isolated points. This was an almost meditative state of alertness, except that it was a matter of life and death.

#### The Hunter-Gatherer Era: Panoramic Scanning and Alertness

Tens of thousands of years ago, on the African savanna, the attention of Homo sapiens was a fluid, open "panoramic view". Survival required them to monitor multiple cues in the environment simultaneously: the rustle of wind and grass, traces of prey, the location of companions, and the approach of predators. This was a highly alert dispersed attention—they had to be ready to switch from "idling" to "stress response" at any time. Modern hunter-gatherer tribes (such as the Hadza people in Tanzania) often sit quietly and gaze for a long time when resting, seemingly in a daze, but actually continuously scanning the horizon. This state is neither the focus of deep reading nor the fragmented switching of scrolling through a phone, but a **diffuse alertness** somewhere in between. It guaranteed survival and also provided the necessary rest space for the brain.

#### The Agricultural Revolution: The Narrowing Gaze and Time Planning

About 10,000 years ago, the agricultural revolution changed all this. When humans settled down and began to sow seeds and measure land, attention was required to "narrow" for the first time. You needed to stare at the seedlings in your field, not the wild fruits on the distant mountains. You needed to remember the abstract agreement on the boundary in the contract. More importantly, agriculture introduced **time planning**—you had to remember when to sow and when to irrigate. This expectation of the future began to reshape the brain's attention mechanism: it no longer just reacted to immediate stimuli, but also maintained focus for long-term goals. The appearance of writing further reinforced this trend. Around 3400 BC, the Sumerians invented cuneiform writing, initially to record agricultural product transactions. Facing the symbols on the clay tablets, people needed to transfer their attention from concrete objects (such as a bag of wheat) to abstract symbols (scratches representing wheat) for the first time. When writing appeared, especially when a person began to face symbols on clay tablets or parchment scrolls, a new mode of attention was born. It required you to block out the birdsong outside the window, the conversation of companions, the touch of the wind, and focus all your spirit on lines of linear, abstract symbols. This is the origin of "linear focus", which trained the brain to enter a deep immersion, at the cost of the gradual fading of the sensory world.

#### The Printing Era: The Golden Age of Deep Reading

In the 15th century, Gutenberg's printing press brought books out of monasteries. Reading was no longer the privilege of monks. Under the kerosene lamp, a person held a book, surrounded by the silent night. That was a deep, lonely immersion. Reading long texts requires sustained attention. You can spend an afternoon following the author's train of thought, climbing mountains, building complex logical castles, and constructing a memory palace that belongs only to you. This ability, which we call the ability of deep reading, directly gave birth to the Scientific Revolution and Enlightenment thought. Historian Elizabeth Eisenstein pointed out in "The Printing Press as an Agent of Change" that printing made knowledge accumulation possible, and scholars could compare different versions of works, discover contradictions, and promote the evolution of thought. The basis of this ability is **long-term, lonely immersion**. It is worth noting that even in that era, deep reading was not the daily life of ordinary people. The literacy rate did not exceed 50% in Europe until the 19th century. Most people still lived in the ocean of oral culture and fragmented information. **So, when we miss "people in the past could calm down and read books", we must admit that it was only the privilege of a few elites, not an ability universally possessed by humans.**

#### The Industrial Revolution: Time Discipline and Commodification of Attention

Then came the long pre-industrial era, where information was always an extremely scarce resource. With the transition of social forms to industrialization, human perception of time was completely reshaped. The gears of the clock cut the natural rhythm that originally flowed with sunrise and sunset into a grid precise to minutes and seconds. Assembly lines and bureaucracies required workers to lock their eyes firmly on the tasks at hand. Attention was forcibly transformed from a loose natural attribute into a core factor of production supporting economic operation. Society began to shape focus into a rule that must be obeyed, and every minute of distraction was equivalent to a loss of efficiency. Homo sapiens learned to discipline their gaze to match the frequency of machine operation. Sociologist E.P. Thompson described in "Time, Work-Discipline, and Industrial Capitalism" that early factory owners forced workers to accept the concept that "time is money" through fines, extending working hours, and other means. Attention was systematically **disciplined** as a factor of production for the first time. At the same time, the increase in literacy rate brought newspapers and novels into public life, and reading began to become a leisure way for the working class—but that was "recovery" outside of work, not the core of work.

#### The Digital Age: The Collision of Infinite Information and Finite Minds

And then, the explosion happened. This was a secret enclosure movement targeting the human mind.

> In 1995, Microsoft released Windows 95, and personal computers began to enter homes. In 2007, the first generation iPhone was released, putting the Internet in everyone's pocket. Since then, in just over a decade, the amount of data produced by humans every day has exceeded the sum of the past few thousand years.

Electronic screens descended, instantly encompassing everything. First was television, which accustomed you to passive reception. Then came the Internet, which piled the information of the entire world on your desktop. Finally, smartphones and algorithmic recommendations completed the final segmentation of your attention. The ancient brain got lost in the fragments of information. Thousands of engineers and behavioral psychologists are dedicated to cracking the underlying reward system of the brain. They quietly implanted random reward mechanisms from psychology into the code. Endless pull-down actions, red notification badges, and push streams accurately calculated by algorithms continuously hijack the release path of dopamine. The nervous system that humans have evolved for millions of years is defenseless against this high-intensity digital stimulation. You can't stand three seconds of boredom because your neural circuits have been trained to expect novel stimulation every moment, and the threshold for stimulation is constantly being raised. A short video has three reversals in one minute, a slap in the face every three episodes, and every blogger is releasing their nature on the other side of the screen. This mode of instantly switching before emotions reach their peak is physically reshaping the brains of a generation. Our brains are gradually getting used to short, intense stimulation and losing the patience to process long and complex information. Even more fatal is that we have lost the ability to endure boredom. Silence and blankness have lost their function as neural buffers and gradually evolved into a state of lack that induces anxiety. The brain has developed a serious intolerance to calmness in continuous high-load operation.

Today, data pours into human optic nerves with unprecedented density. The huge business empires built by technology giants have their core assets in every second that humans stay in front of the screen. Individual attention is ruthlessly cut into fine fragments, converted into data points in servers, and finally packaged and sold in the trading market. In this process, the cognitive structure of Homo sapiens has undergone profound changes. Staring at a tree in a daze requires absolute control over self-consciousness. We seem to have a portable terminal connecting all human knowledge, but we hand over the sovereignty of our minds in every unconscious swipe of the screen. This war on attention has no smoke in the physical sense; it happens at the moment every morning when we wake up and reach for our phones. When we try to pull our gaze out of the glowing screen and cast it back to the surrounding physical reality, the difficult exploration of how to regain sovereignty over the mind has just begun.

This is no longer just a story about self-control, but a war. The battlefield is the three pounds of soft tissue behind your forehead. The warring parties are your intention and curiosity as an independent individual on one side, and the most sophisticated attention harvesting system of the entire digital economy on the other. We are not trying to be retro and nostalgic, retreating to the savanna without writing. History cannot go backwards, and we cannot smash our phones (after all, they didn't come from the wind). But when we realize that this ability of deep gaze, the ability to stare at a tree for a long time until seeing its texture, its story, and even its soul, is disappearing from the human group on a large scale, this becomes a signal worth being vigilant about.

### Final Words

This article might be longer than you expected. If you have persisted in reading until here, it means your attention system has not completely fallen. But I also know clearly that many people will be interrupted by other things halfway, or exit because they feel bored—this is perfectly normal.

The important thing is that we don't have to blame ourselves for this, nor do we need to nostalgically think that the past was so beautiful. Every mode of attention is an adaptation to the environment, and the environment never stops changing. What we can do is to understand the mechanism of this change, see clearly who is trying to manipulate our minds, and then make our own choice: to drift with the tide, or to deliberately reserve a part of our time for depth, boredom, and silence. In the coming era of comprehensive artificial intelligence, when machines can process most information and shallow logic, the scarce deep focus and the insights generated from it preserved by humans may become the invisible line that divides the future.

Or perhaps, this itself doesn't mean much. All insights will be drowned by massive amounts of information, and our only remaining value is to provide precious attention to content, filter out new content that attracts more attention, and then cycle back and forth. **After all, we are the only content consumers on this planet, right?**
