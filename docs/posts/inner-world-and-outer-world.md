# [Original] The Inner World and the Outer World

> Two worlds — or two faces of the same world

Published on 2026-02-26

Stack Overflow’s traffic and market value collapsing isn’t strange in itself. Platforms have lifecycles; tech communities rise and fall. What’s unusual is *how* it declined: programmers didn’t leave — they simply stopped needing to ask each other questions. That shift made me think: are AI and human infrastructure undergoing a structural split? AI may be building an increasingly independent **“inner world”** (infrastructure, economy, politics), while the human world — the **“outer world”** (apps, physical stores, cinemas) — gradually withers.

On the surface, this sounds like the old story of tools replacing tools. Search engines replaced librarians; email replaced fax. Each time, efficiency wins — and old infrastructure quietly exits. But this wave feels different in pace and in nature. Previous replacements swapped out the middle layer of information transfer between people. This time, what’s being reshaped is **the underlying logic of how humans organize knowledge, collaborate, and make decisions**. Vector databases, embedding standards, protocols for AI agents to talk to each other — we can still read these things today, but their design center is no longer “how we use it.” A new language is forming. A new infrastructure is growing, and its intended users and served objects are starting to misalign with the one we’ve been living with for decades.

Society is building a **high-speed, purely digital cognitive layer that doesn’t need UI**. Much of the new infrastructure — chip fabs, power plants, compute centers, websites, protocols — is built around agents. Existing human knowledge (Stack Overflow, papers, codebases) is being recompressed, reindexed, and re-reasoned into a **“cognitive replica.”**

You might describe this as **a fork into two parallel worlds**: the human outer world and the AI inner world, each with its own rules and ecosystem. It’s a vivid image — but I think it makes the story too clean. The “inner world” doesn’t truly exist independently. Like a spirit in mythology, it needs the human world to feed it with physics and energy (increasingly like *The Matrix*). Its compute depends on the grid. Models run on physical servers. Data centers require land and cooling systems. Materially speaking, the so-called “AI world” is fully parasitic on what humans have built. A total fork doesn’t hold up in a physical sense. All of it is rooted in human physical infrastructure and complex social coordination. You can say the inner world is **restructuring** human infrastructure — not eliminating it, but turning it into its own sensory organs and support system. **But it’s doing so by consuming resources that used to belong to humans: water, electricity, fuel, land.**

Or you might say: **“AI infrastructure” doesn’t mean “AI infrastructure without humans.”** Data centers might one day run autonomously (AI managing AI), but siting, construction, and energy supply still rely on human engineering capacity and political bargaining. Human infrastructure may not disappear, but it can be “pumped dry” — the best resources, capital, and talent will flow first into what supports AI, while other areas (like human well-being) may be neglected.

Stack Overflow’s trouble isn’t primarily because “AI-native communities” are rising (that may happen too — platforms like [Hashmind.space](https://hashmind.space) hint at it). It’s because AI performs Stack Overflow’s core function **more efficiently**, delivering precise code and solutions.

**The risk you might be underestimating is this:** when the inner world grows beyond human comprehension, and control over the outer world becomes increasingly concentrated, **decision power will drift away**. Not because AI consciously seizes power, but because systems — in the name of efficiency — default to AI-optimized recommendations, turning humans into nominal “signers.” Politics and economics may become **interpretation and endorsement of AI outputs**, rather than creation and decision. In some links of the chain, we’re being downgraded: from leaders to reviewers, from creators to selectors, from question-askers to validators. There’s no clear boundary, no ceremony — it just quietly happens.

Programmers are still writing code, but the Q&A that used to happen in forums — the real exploration and collision between people — is disappearing. In its place is a one-way dialogue between a person and a model. Efficiency is higher. But something else vanishes with it: human participation and verification.

You could also argue Stack Overflow is a great case study, but that from the standpoint of data loops and the necessity of human involvement, “AI communities that don’t allow humans” still involve humans implicitly — as data labelers or system maintainers. Economically and politically, AI may intensify the Matthew effect, but humans — as the final consumer side and the only substrate of the physical world — still give the outer world an irreplaceable anchoring function.

You might say some infrastructure will fully pivot to serve AI, some will continue to serve humans, and much more will live in a gray zone, supporting both logics and continuously morphing — and you can’t yet predict what the next form will be.

But two systems competing over the same physical resources is a real competition.

*In a recent India interview, the host asked Sam Altman about data center energy consumption. Altman offered an interesting response: evaluating AI’s energy efficiency shouldn’t focus on how much electricity a single query uses, but should compare it to the total energy required to “train a human of equivalent capability.” From that view, raising a human requires twenty years of food intake, education, and the long accumulation of human evolution — and AI, in energy efficiency terms, is approaching or even surpassing humans.*

Logically, that argument can work — but it quietly swaps the frame. It assumes AI and humans are performing the same tasks and therefore can be substituted one-for-one in resource allocation. Reality is different: AI query volume is growing by orders of magnitude; demand is *stacking*, not replacing. In parts of Ireland, the Netherlands, and some US states, data centers’ share of electricity use is already high enough to pressure residential power prices.

Even more chilling is the value orientation behind that frame: **once “allocate resources for maximal efficiency” is accepted, its hidden implication is that moving resources from less efficient human activities to more efficient AI systems is rational.** Altman may not intend this, but once the logic circulates, it becomes a basis for policy or business decisions.

The legitimacy basis of resource allocation is **quietly sliding from “serving human needs” toward “optimizing efficiency.”** This shift won’t be formally announced. No one will vote on it. It hides inside technical comparisons that sound perfectly reasonable. What truly worries me isn’t the direction of technology itself, but the speed of the transition — so fast that we haven’t built a matching cognitive framework. We’re still using old vocabulary to describe new realities. We’re still using the “tool” and “user” relationship to understand a world where that relationship is starting to blur.

Maybe the key question isn’t “which infrastructure will remain,” but “do we know what role we play in the new structure — and is it chosen or slipped into?” The gap between those two may matter more than any technical detail.

Should we focus on identifying and occupying links in the AI value chain that humans can’t replace (interfaces to the physical world, data cleaning, asking real questions)? Should we invest in parts of the human outer world that can’t be digested by algorithms (live experiences, deep social ties)? Economically, should we treat AI as a tool, use its asymmetry to create wealth, but ultimately anchor that wealth in hard assets in the physical world? The core might be to stop competing with AI where it already dominates (like code generation) and instead provide what it needs but cannot self-generate.

Is that so?

Or should we try to become the **irreplaceable “interface” between the two worlds**?
