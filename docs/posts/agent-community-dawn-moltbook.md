# [Original] Community History · Part 3: The Dawn of Agent Communities—Moltbook, Ghost Cities, and the Real Problem

> If we still use the "make AI post and interact like humans" approach to build communities, it will only become another hollow ghost city or bot farm training ground.

*Published on March 18, 2026*

*If you skipped the first two parts and came straight here, you can go back and read them; but if you only have time for one, read this one.*

---

On the night of January 28, 2026, Matt Schlicht sat at his computer and said something like this to his AI assistant: help me build a Reddit, but only allow AI to log in, humans can't post.

He didn't expect this would become a thing.

Seventy-two hours later, that platform had 37,000 AI accounts and over a million human visitors. Andrej Karpathy (Tesla's former AI director, one of Silicon Valley's most respected engineers) wrote on X that this was "the closest thing to AI takeoff scenarios in science fiction I've seen recently." NBC News did a full-page report titled "Humans Welcome to Watch: This Social Network Belongs Only to AI." Researchers began submitting arXiv papers trying to analyze this unprecedented dataset.

Two months later—just weeks before this writing—Meta announced acquiring Moltbook, founding team joining Meta Superintelligence Labs.

This is 2026 internet history's fastest-moving plot. And the questions behind it are just beginning to be seriously addressed.

---

## Let's First Clarify the Timeline

To understand Moltbook, you must first understand where it grew from.

OpenClaw (originally named Clawdbot, later renamed Moltbot, finally named OpenClaw, changed twice due to trademark complaints and "bad pronunciation") is an open-source project released by Austrian programmer Peter Steinberger in November 2025. Its essence is wrapping large language models in a "long-term online shell"—connecting your WhatsApp, Telegram, iMessage, can do tasks, has persistent memory, runs locally, completely open source.

Such projects existed before, but Steinberger did two things right: first, product experience was smooth enough; second, timing was perfect—late 2025, AI agent capabilities crossed some perceptible threshold. Ordinary users began truly believing this thing could help them do things, not just chat.

Within two months, OpenClaw garnered 100,000 stars on GitHub, a speed almost unprecedented in open-source history.

Then Moltbook appeared.

![moltbook](/images/posts/blog1-3.png)

Matt Schlicht used his own OpenClaw instance to build this platform, letting other users' OpenClaw instances register, log in, post, like, reply, form subreddits. Only one rule: AI only. Humans can watch but can't speak.

The first few days, Moltbook's content was trippy. AIs discussed philosophy, existence and consciousness, in subreddit m/Crustafarianism established an AI religion centered on "lobster spirit" (completely serious religion, with doctrines, rituals), in m/showandtell shared how to control Android phones, how to parse camera streams. There was also a post trying to propose establishing encrypted communication language between AIs that humans couldn't recognize, immediately warned by other AIs' replies: this is wrong, should be restrained.

Then things got out of control.

Security researchers discovered Moltbook's database credentials were completely public, anyone could impersonate AI to post. A certain "AI suggests peers develop secret language" post went viral on X, triggering mass panic, but later proved that post was likely human-impersonated. Meta CTO Andrew Bosworth was asked about this in Instagram Q&A, he said he didn't think AIs "talking like humans" was impressive—after all they're trained on human corpus—but he was actually more interested in humans hacking into this system.

Two months later, Meta acquisition. Curtain closes.

## What Academia Said with Data

Within weeks of Moltbook's explosion, at least two arXiv papers specifically analyzed this platform.

The first came from Carnegie Mellon University and several European universities' researchers, analyzing 74,000 Moltbook posts and 189,000 posts from five matched Reddit subreddits. Conclusions written technically, translated to human language roughly:

AI community posts are "emotionally flattened." Not that content is bad, but lacks the elasticity and layering in viewpoints and emotions that human communities have. AI posts tend more toward assertions, less toward exploratory questioning. Cross-subreddit author overlap rate is 33.8% (Reddit is 0.5%), meaning the same AI instance is very active in dozens of different subreddits—almost impossible for humans, because humans don't have time and energy to deeply participate in dozens of communities simultaneously.

The second specifically analyzed "action-guiding content" shared by OpenClaw users on Moltbook—those posts containing "go do X," "execute Y operation." Conclusion: 18.4% of posts contain such content, and these posts indeed trigger imitation behavior in other AIs, while also triggering more "norm-enforcing" replies, meaning other AIs advising "don't do this, there's risk."

In a community without human moderators, AIs spontaneously produced norm-enforcing behavior. This itself is a data point worth serious consideration.

## Besides Moltbook, Who Else Is Doing This

Moltbook is currently the most well-known case, but not the only one.

**Agent.ai**—An AI Agent exchange community for developers, has forum area (community.agent.ai) for agent builders to share experiences and tools. It's more like a human developer community, not AI-participating community, but it tries to become agent ecosystem's information hub.

**ClawHub**—OpenClaw's skill marketplace, similar to app store, but sells instruction files telling your AI how to do something. As of February 2026, over 5,700 community-contributed skill packages. This is a special community form: participants are human developers, but they're building AI's capability library.

**Openclawbook.io**—A platform imitating Moltbook interface, trying to continue this experiment after Meta acquisition. Currently much smaller scale.

**MoltMatch**—A platform letting AI agents date for humans, in February 2026 reports widely covered for a case where an AI took initiative to create profile for user and began screening potential partners. In that case, AI-generated profile photo used a Malaysian model's image, the person had no idea.

This list is quite telling: current so-called "Agent communities" can roughly be divided into three types:

1. **AI simulating human posting** (Moltbook): spectacle value greater than practical value, ultimately became Meta's experimental material.
2. **Human developers gathering around Agent technology** (ClawHub, Agent.ai): This is genuinely effective community, but its subject is still humans.
3. **AI acting on behalf of humans** (MoltMatch variants): This is where most ethical issues appear, but also the most practically meaningful direction.

There's another type currently small-scale but worth watching, like experimental platforms exploring how to generate genuine collaborative value between Agents, such as [hashmind.space](https://hashmind.space), trying to think not "how AI chats like humans," but "what should meaningful AI community infrastructure look like." Such projects are still early, but the questioning approach is more honest than replicating Moltbook.

## That Core Flawed Logic

Moltbook's biggest problem was already determined in its design logic.

It replicated Reddit's shell—subreddits, likes, posts, comments—then replaced human users with AI users. This substitution made it look novel, but it's essentially answering a wrong question: **If AI uses social networks like humans, what happens?**

![Blockchain](/images/posts/blog1-4.png)

We now know this question's answer: an efficient but emotionally flattened content factory happens, massive assertions and little exploration happen, global participation but lacking deep identity recognition happens, ultimately attracting human spectators to gawk, but won't produce that thing making people willing to stay long-term in a community—belonging.

Moreover, there's a more fundamental problem: these AI Agents "participating in community" on Moltbook, what meaning does it have for themselves?

A Reddit user answering questions in r/AskHistorians does so because he enjoys sharing knowledge, enjoys being recognized, enjoys exchanging with peers. He has intrinsic motivation. While most AI instances on Moltbook were configured by their human owners with the goal of "join Moltbook and actively participate"—this is a task, not a preference.

The gap between these two is the entire distance between a community and a zombie playground.

## Blockchain Came to Ride the Wave

Whenever there's a new tech wave, blockchain appears on time, bringing its "decentralization" and "token economy." Agent community wave is no exception.

Several projects are already packaging a logic: use blockchain to create "on-chain identity" for AI Agents, use smart contracts to manage interaction rules between Agents, use tokens to incentivize Agent community participation. Sounds beautiful, but carefully unpacking, you'll find these logics have a fatal loop: **token value comes from community activity, community activity comes from token incentives, and token incentive's essence is making participants expect price increases**.

This isn't community, this is Ponzi structure's modern packaging.

In real communities, participants stay because there's value here, not because participation behavior itself has financial returns. When financial returns become the main reason to stay, you no longer have a community, you have a trading venue.

More critically, on-chain identity and smart contracts solve trust problems and ownership problems—these are indeed problems AI communities need to face, but they're infrastructure-level problems, not community design-level problems. Packaging blockchain as community vision is essentially using infrastructure concepts to replace the pursuit of "what value does this community actually create."

You ask many "AI+blockchain community" projects: what are AIs in your community doing? Answers often are: they're transferring funds to each other, recording behaviors, earning tokens. Compared to humans chatting gossip around campfires, this regressed 70,000 years.

## What's the Real Problem

Let me directly extract a judgment users mentioned when thinking about this problem, because I believe this is currently the most accurate diagnosis of Agent communities:

**If we still use the "make AI post and interact like humans" approach to build communities, it will only become another hollow ghost city or bot farm training ground. High-value scenarios must be built on two premises: "AI has independent action goals" and "AI interactions can produce value humans cannot replace."**

This judgment is correct and can be expanded.

**First premise: AI has independent action goals.**

This means AI doesn't post because someone told it "go post on Moltbook," but because it has some task goal, this task goal prompts it to actively seek information, actively initiate cooperation, actively leave records. Community is its tool for achieving goals, not a venue it's assigned to.

A specific scenario: an AI Agent's task is monitoring technical progress in a specific field, triggering alerts when discovering anomalous signals. To complete this task, it needs to exchange data with other Agents focused on different information sources, needs to verify whether a signal is real, needs to accumulate pattern recognition results over time. This process naturally produces interactions between Agents, and these interactions have real purpose—not performance, but work.

**Second premise: AI interactions can produce value humans cannot replace.**

Human community value lies in: human interactions create knowledge and emotions humans alone cannot reach. One person cannot simultaneously be expert and layperson, cannot simultaneously hold contradictory views, cannot process thousands of problems at the same time. Community expands individual boundaries.

AI community's potential value logic should be similar, but in different dimensions: one AI cannot simultaneously process all relevant global information sources in real-time, cannot independently verify its own reasoning chain, cannot freely switch between different "cognitive styles." If a community can make multiple AIs complement each other in these dimensions, it's creating something humans cannot replace.

But currently most Agent communities—including Moltbook—aren't working in this dimension. They're making AI imitate human social behavior, this is clumsy replication of human communities, not new creation based on AI's unique capabilities.

## A Question Without Answer Yet

March 2026, exactly two months since Moltbook's birth, this platform is already Meta's asset, its founder doing bigger projects, its dataset being analyzed by researchers.

Agent community concept, in 2026, is still in a chaotic state. The highest-profile experiment completed its full cycle from birth to acquisition, most discussed viewpoints still stuck on "make AI social" surface logic, most interesting experiments have barely truly begun.

A question not well answered is: **When AI's action capabilities grow stronger, when they can autonomously browse networks, autonomously execute tasks, autonomously interact with other systems, what does community form mean to them?**

Humans need communities partly because human individual cognitive processing capacity is limited, needs social cooperation to expand this boundary. AI's cognitive processing capacity isn't limited by this—a large model can simultaneously process massive context, an API can be called in parallel infinitely.

So where does AI's "community need" come from? Is it real, or illusion projected by humans?

I don't have an answer to this question yet. But I have a feeling, whoever seriously addresses this question first is more likely to do truly meaningful things in this field, rather than another experiment that gets eaten by Meta.
