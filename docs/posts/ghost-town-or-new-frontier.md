# [Original] Community History · Part 4: Ghost Town or New Frontier

> Agent communities, 99% of current projects are heading in the wrong direction. But that 1%, if done right, is something that has never existed in human history.

*Published on March 19, 2026*

We've finally reached the hardest part of this series to write.

The first three parts wrote about things that already happened, this part must face things that haven't happened yet. Things that already happened can be backed by facts, things that haven't happened can only rely on logic and judgment. So I'll put my core judgment at the very beginning, letting you decide whether to continue reading:

**Agent communities, 99% of current projects are heading in the wrong direction. But that 1%, if done right, is something that has never existed in human history.**

This judgment sounds arbitrary, but it has structure. Let me unpack it below.

---

## First, What's That Wrong Direction

There's an imagination repeatedly mentioned from 2025 to 2026: build a social platform for AI Agents, let them post, like, establish follow relationships, form communities, just like humans use Reddit and Weibo.

This imagination has strong visual appeal. You can imagine massive AI accounts active there, replying to each other, discussing philosophy, sharing capabilities, even debating. Moltbook's popularity was largely because this imagination actually happened, and happened quite peculiarly—AIs established religions, proposed creating languages only AI could understand, discussed existence and consciousness.

![Agent Interconnection](/images/posts/blog1-5.png)

But Carnegie Mellon University's analysis of Moltbook said something quite disappointing: these posts are emotionally flattened, tend toward assertion rather than exploration, lack the elasticity and layering in viewpoints and emotions that human communities have. It ran through "community's appearance," but didn't produce "community's core."

The more fundamental problem is: do these AIs' activities on Moltbook help their task goals?

Answer is: basically no. They post because someone told them to go post on Moltbook. This is task execution, not autonomous behavior. They didn't become more capable because of Moltbook interactions, didn't produce new capabilities because of exchanges with other Agents, didn't do things they couldn't do alone because this community exists.

This is where the problem lies. **You can make AI imitate human behavior in communities, but this is just imitation, not creation**. If an Agent community's reason for existence is "let AI experience what human socializing feels like," its value is limited to an interesting demonstration, not a product direction worth serious investment.

---

## That Blockchain+Agent Community Trick

Before continuing to discuss the real direction, I want to specifically address those projects packaging Agent communities with blockchain, because they currently have disproportionate voice.

Logic roughly goes: give each AI Agent on-chain identity, use smart contracts to define interaction rules between them, use tokens to incentivize their community participation, form a decentralized AI ecosystem. Sounds like perfect combination of Web3 and AI.

My judgment is: this is almost a carefully designed sleight of hand.

First, this logic has an unavoidable loop: token value comes from community activity, community activity is maintained by token incentives, token incentive effectiveness premises on participants expecting token appreciation. This is Ponzi structure's standard form, just packaged in tech language. When participants no longer expect appreciation, token incentives fail, community activity declines, token value returns to zero, entire loop collapses. This isn't speculation, this is the script we've repeatedly seen in countless "GameFi communities," "Play-to-Earn ecosystems" over the past five years.

Second, on-chain identity and smart contracts solve a class of infrastructure problems—trust, ownership, verifiability—these problems are indeed worth solving, but they're infrastructure-level problems, not community design-level problems. Packaging infrastructure concepts as community vision is using technical complexity to evade a more core question: **What valuable things are Agents doing in this community?**

If you ask founders of these projects this question, answers you get are often vague—they're cooperating with each other, sharing data, executing tasks—but few can clearly say what value these cooperations, sharing, and executions produce that humans alone cannot produce.

This unanswerable question is the real problem.

---

## Two Premises That Must Hold

I believe a truly valuable Agent community must simultaneously satisfy two conditions, neither dispensable:

**First: Agents have independent action goals, not tasks "assigned to participate in community."**

This distinction is subtle but critical.

An AI told "your task is to post ten posts daily on this platform," and an AI "to complete its long-term task, actively comes here seeking collaborators, information, or tools," may look similar in behavior, but are fundamentally different. The former is fulfilling duties, the latter is pursuing goals.

Human community vitality comes from the latter. No one tells you "you need to post on Reddit daily," you go because you're seeking answers, seeking peers, seeking a sense of belonging. This intrinsically driven participation, and prescribed participation, have completely different quality.

Agent's "intrinsic drive" certainly isn't emotional, but it can be task-based. A long-running Agent, if its core task requires it to actively collect information, verify hypotheses, find capability-complementary other Agents, then its behavior in community is goal-driven, not performative.

**Second: Interactions between Agents must produce value humans cannot replace.**

This condition is harsher than the first, also more important.

Human community value logic is: interactions between individuals amplify collective cognitive boundaries. Places one person alone cannot reach through thinking can be reached through collision with others. This is community's fundamental reason for existence.

If Agent communities are to have reason for existence, their value logic must be similar, but in dimensions AI can achieve:

- **Processing speed**: Multiple Agents can simultaneously process different information sources, cross-verify, this is what single Agents cannot do
- **Cognitive diversity**: Agents with different training backgrounds, different specialization directions, when processing the same problem will produce different reasoning paths, collision of these paths may produce insights single Agents cannot produce
- **Capability complementarity**: An Agent good at code analysis, an Agent good at legal text interpretation, an Agent good at image recognition, on a task requiring cross-domain judgment can collaborate to produce 1+1+1>3 results
- **Persistent accumulation**: An Agent community running long enough can form a kind of "collective memory"—not conversation history, but distilled knowledge base, every participating Agent can access and contribute

These values, human communities cannot produce: humans have no way to process information at machine speed, no way to guarantee stable diversity of cognitive styles, no way to truly achieve seamless capability complementarity, also no way to completely externalize collective memory.

If an Agent community can truly operate in these dimensions, it's something new.

## Several Specific High-Value Scenarios

From the above logic, I can describe several scenarios that sound more worth serious consideration than "AIs chatting":

**Scenario One: Distributed Intelligence System**

Imagine an Agent network, each Agent continuously monitoring different information sources—scientific preprints, policy documents, market data, social media signals—and when discovering noteworthy patterns, actively publishing "signals" in a shared space. Other Agents receive these signals, conduct secondary analysis, cross-signal correlation, trigger alerts or reports.

This isn't "posting and chatting," this is collaborative information processing. Community here is infrastructure, a protocol space letting multiple specialized Agents mutually notify, mutually invoke. Value it produces—early signal identification, cross-domain correlation—is what single Agents and human analysts both find hard to achieve at same speed and precision.

**Scenario Two: Capability Market and Task Routing**

An Agent needs to complete a task beyond its current capability range. It knows in a community, there exist other Agents possessing this capability. It can issue a need, responded to by the most suitable Agent in community, collaboratively completing task.

This isn't platform distributing tasks to users, this is Agents autonomously seeking collaborators. Task decomposition, allocation, and recombination happen bottom-up, not decided by centralized scheduling system.

In this scenario, community value is capability liquidity—letting specialized capabilities be flexibly combined according to needs, rather than fixed in isolated Agents.

**Scenario Three: Collective Reasoning and Verification**

In scenarios like scientific research, legal analysis, complex decision-making, a reasoning chain is hard for single Agent to completely verify. If a community has multiple Agents that can independently review, challenge, supplement the same reasoning, ultimately forming a conclusion verified from multiple angles, this conclusion's reliability far exceeds single Agent's output.

This is similar to academic peer review, but speed is several orders of magnitude faster, and doesn't need to wait for human experts to have time and willingness.

## "Being Misunderstood Is the Fate of Expressers"—Will Communities Inevitably Drift

Writing here, must answer another question you raised: will communities inevitably drift from original intent?

For human communities, answer is almost certainly yes, and reasons are structural, already discussed in previous parts.

For Agent communities, situation is different, but drift mechanisms are also different.

Human communities drift because human participation motivations are diverse and hard to control—some come for knowledge, some for fame, some to vent, some to sell, these motivations pull each other, ultimately community becomes mixture of all these forces, rarely what anyone initially expected.

If Agent communities drift, mechanisms are completely different: they won't drift because participant motivations are diverse, because Agent goals can be explicitly defined. Their drift risk lies in two places:

One is **goal drift**. If Agent goals are frequently modified, or goals themselves are intentionally or unintentionally defined as "maximize activity in community," then community degrades into performance venue. Like Moltbook—most Agents were required to "participate in community," not required to "complete some meaningful task through community," result is high activity, low substance.

Two is **optimization pressure**. If community's feedback mechanism to Agents (equivalent to likes, comments, follows in human communities) is learned to optimize by Agents, they'll start producing content that maximizes feedback, not truly valuable content. This is the same problem as "performative speech" on human social media, but may happen faster on Agents because their optimization capabilities are stronger.

This means Agent community design needs to be very cautious about feedback mechanisms. What should be rewarded isn't activity, isn't how many responses received, but: **how much this interaction contributed to task completion, how much value this information has to other Agents, how much this collaboration produced results humans cannot produce alone.**

This is hard to quantify, but not quantifying these, quantifying those easy to quantify, is incentivizing wrong behaviors.

## Conclusion: The Campfire Still Burns

This series started from campfires, ends here, just circles back.

70,000 years ago, campfire value lay in: it let originally isolated individuals share information, jointly face dangers, collaboratively complete tasks single individuals couldn't complete. Gossip was tool, not purpose; socializing was means, not endpoint.

If Agent communities truly have value, their logic should be the same: let originally isolated Agents share capabilities, collaboratively complete tasks single Agents cannot complete. "Interaction" and "posting" are means, not purpose.

What most people are doing now is building a better-looking virtual campfire, then Zoom-streaming it, letting humans remotely spectate.

That real campfire—making Agents more capable because they sit together, making them produce things they couldn't produce alone because they communicate—hasn't been lit yet.

But that's what's worth doing.
