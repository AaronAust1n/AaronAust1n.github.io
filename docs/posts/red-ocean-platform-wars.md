# [Original] Community History · Part 2: Platform Wars in the Red Ocean

> To defend, you copy competitors; copying competitors, you lose yourself; losing yourself, what you're truly defending—community temperament—also disappears.

*Published on March 17, 2026*

If we make a chart, horizontal axis is user age, vertical axis is daily usage time, plotting Douyin, WeChat, Weibo, Kuaishou, Xiaohongshu, Zhihu all on the same coordinate system. It illustrates something simple: these platforms' users highly overlap. The same person has three or four such apps on their phone, drifting between them daily. This isn't an incremental market, this is slicing cake.

![Douyin](/images/posts/blog2.png)

China's internet community track completed its transformation from blue ocean to red ocean roughly between 2015-2018. During that period, mobile internet user penetration crossed its peak, new users no longer popped out of stone cracks but had to be snatched from competitors. The most direct consequence of this shift was all platforms began doing something they previously disdained: **eating each other's territory**.

---

## Douyin Wants Zhihu's, Kuaishou Wants Xiaohongshu's

In 2021, Douyin launched "Douyin Encyclopedia," later also launched "Q&A" functionality. ByteDance's another product Wukong Q&A directly poached Zhihu's top answerers, reportedly packaging 300 "Big Vs" at once, offering up to tens of millions in signing fees. This poaching operation was described in the industry as "naked plunder," but Zhihu was almost powerless to fight back because those answerers had too limited monetization methods on Zhihu.

Zhihu's response was: attempting video-ification. They launched "video answers" functionality, encouraging users to convert text content into videos. This was a very typical passive following—because competitors gained advantage in video, they ran over to do video, but casually destroyed their core user experience. Zhihu users opened the app, saw screens full of video content, that expectation of "I can find serious text answers here" shattered, so some people left.

This is a repeatedly appearing paradox in platform wars: **To defend, you copy competitors; copying competitors, you lose yourself; losing yourself, what you're truly defending—community temperament—also disappears.**

Douyin eats Zhihu through capital and feature replication. Weibo eats WeChat by copying Stories mode. Xiaohongshu eats Weibo through precise occupation of female users. Kuaishou eats Douyin through lower-tier market human networks. Behind each attack line is precise identification of "what they have that I don't," then补齐 actions at any cost.

ByteDance is the fiercest hunter in this war, also the most undisguised in intent. Toutiao is content aggregation, Douyin is short video, Feishu is enterprise collaboration, Duoshan is instant messaging, Wukong Q&A is knowledge Q&A, Pipixia is image-text community—this isn't product planning, this is systematic territory occupation, trying industry by industry, hitting wherever user density exists.

But ByteDance has one thing it never succeeded at: **instant messaging**. Duoshan died, Feiliao died, Feishu has decent penetration in enterprise market, but in mass social field, WeChat's moat remains unbreakable. This shows platform wars have boundaries—in some fields, network effects formed by first-mover advantage are so strong that latecomers can almost never overcome them, no matter how much money you have.

---

## How Xiaohongshu Became That Strange Exception

In this platform war, Xiaohongshu's approach is the least war-like.

It didn't actively attack anyone, what it did was: **make itself something others can't easily replicate**.

Xiaohongshu's moat isn't technology, isn't algorithms, isn't capital—its moat is a community atmosphere hard to quantify with metrics. That feeling of "sharing here is sincere, someone is seriously watching" is Xiaohongshu's core asset, also its most fragile asset.

ByteDance tried replicating Xiaohongshu with "Douyin Life Circle," did it for a while, data didn't look good, quietly scaled back. Taobao has "Guang Guang," also didn't truly threaten Xiaohongshu. Reason is simple: Xiaohongshu's content ecosystem accumulated over years, formed real community density in core verticals like beauty, travel, lifestyle, new entrants need time to reach equivalent density, and time is almost unbuyable in this speed market.

But Xiaohongshu's threat isn't just external competition, more so internal deterioration.

When commercial content massively floods in, when the boundary between brand placements and user genuine sharing becomes increasingly blurred, Xiaohongshu's core asset—"authenticity feeling"—begins slowly eroding. Users begin using another reading strategy to process Xiaohongshu content: first assume it's advertising, then see if there's real experience mixed in. This reading method itself is a symptom of declining trust.

What Xiaohongshu is doing now is finding balance between commercialization and community feeling, this balance is never stable, always needs recalibration. Mao Wenchao emphasizes "authenticity" in various occasions, but "emphasizing authenticity" itself shows authenticity has become a scarce good needing maintenance, not a taken-for-granted foundation.

## Kuaishou's Egalitarian Philosophy, and Why It Didn't Persist

Kuaishou is the most interestingly conceived of this generation's platforms, in my opinion.

Its early algorithm design had an obsession with "fairness." Not just giving traffic to top creators, but actively distributing exposure to mid-tier and bottom-tier users, letting every ordinary person have a chance to be seen. This is quite rare in internet products, because it contradicts usual commercial logic—most platforms concentrate traffic on most eye-catching content, because this maximizes retention and ad revenue.

Kuaishou said no, I want to let all "laotie" (buddies) speak.

This decision produced a unique content ecosystem: Kuaishou has massive amounts of content recording ordinary people's real lives, a Northeast fishing village elder sharing his fishing videos, a Guangxi rural girl recording her daily life, this content is hard to be seen on other platforms, on Kuaishou has real audiences, people liking, commenting, saying "my home is like this too." This is the internet truly fulfilling its original promise for a brief moment: letting everyone have a voice.

Then Kuaishou went public, benchmarked against Douyin, pressure came.

Egalitarian algorithm was quietly adjusted, traffic began tilting toward top tier, because this makes DAU data look better, ad revenue more efficient. Laotie culture's base color remains, but that egalitarian logic is no longer core.

This is an almost inevitable trajectory after internet community commercialization: **Platforms start with some genuine community promise, grow by violating that promise to exchange for scale efficiency**. Users stay because of inertia, because social relationships are already here, because switching platforms costs too much, not because that original promise is still being fulfilled.

## Weibo's Midlife Crisis

Weibo is the earliest to reach ceiling among this batch of platforms, also most candidly displaying "broadcast platform's structural limitations."

Its user count peaked around 2018, then began slowly declining. But the more fundamental problem isn't user count, but its content structure. Weibo's core traffic is always concentrated on celebrity gossip and a few opinion leaders, ordinary users' participation feeling is extremely weak—what you post, only your few followers can see, and among your followers, half are accounts that haven't been active since following years ago.

This is broadcast media's essential problem: it creates audiences, not participants.

Weibo made massive efforts to solve this problem: Super Topics (cutting follow graphs into interest communities), video accounts, live streaming, but these efforts didn't fundamentally change its power structure. You open Weibo, first thing you see is still those few Big Vs' content, still some celebrity trending again today, this logic shapes users into consumers, not community members.

One data point worth mentioning: among Weibo's daily active users, the proportion actually posting remains in single digits year-round. Most people open Weibo to watch, not to speak. This is a healthy media product, but not a healthy community product.

## Things Wasted in Stock Market Wars

Platform wars have a cost rarely seriously discussed: they force all participants to invest resources in feature replication, not in genuine innovation.

When Douyin does Q&A, it's because Zhihu is doing knowledge monetization; when Xiaohongshu does live streaming, it's because Taobao is doing content e-commerce; when Weibo does short video, it's because Douyin Kuaishou are dividing time. Every platform is simultaneously doing three things: defending its core, attacking others' cores, defending against others attacking its core. This is an extremely resource-consuming state, and it consumes not just money, but attention, team energy, and product soul.

In this state, truly meaningful product innovation is almost impossible. You don't have mental energy to think "what should communities look like in five years," you can only think "what features did competitors launch this quarter, should I follow."

WeChat is one of the few exceptions—it long maintained an almost unbelievably restrained posture, didn't turn itself into a feed product, didn't immediately replicate Douyin when short video exploded, but launched Video Accounts at its own pace. This restraint comes from WeChat's unique moat (instant messaging's social graph), and Zhang Xiaolong's certain persistence in product philosophy, but how long this persistence can last depends on Tencent's overall strategic pressure.

## What's the Endgame of Social Red Ocean

No one truly knows.
![Connection](/images/posts/blog1-4.png)
But one scenario is increasingly worth serious consideration: **When AI can generate customized content in real-time based on user preferences, when AI can replace real users completing massive community interactions, what happens to existing platforms' "community feeling" foundation?**

Xiaohongshu's "authenticity feeling," once AI-generated planting posts can pass as real and be injected at scale, dies directly. Zhihu's "knowledge authority feeling," once AI answer quality stably exceeds ordinary answerers, loses core attraction. Kuaishou's "laotie human touch" is currently hardest for AI to replicate, because it's built on real geography and real social relationships, but this is also just a matter of time.

Platforms' responses to this threat currently mainly have two types: one is embracing AI as content production tool, allowing even encouraging AI-generated content, exchanging for content volume growth; another is emphasizing "real human creation," making human creation a quality label, marking or even restricting AI-generated content.

Both directions can work, but both have costs. Embracing AI content accelerates community temperament dilution; emphasizing human creation falls behind in content volume, and in AI capability rapid improvement stage, this defense line is increasingly hard to hold.

Stock market red ocean wars continue, but the battlefield is undergoing a more fundamental shift. Original war was platforms competing for human users' time and attention; next war is platforms and AI competing for definitional power over the concept of "authenticity."

Whoever can make users believe "this place is authentic" has core moat. This thing, in 2026, is harder to achieve than ever, also more valuable than ever.
